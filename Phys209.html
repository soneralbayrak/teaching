What is this course?

For a formal answer to this question, see

https://catalog.metu.edu.tr/course.php?course_code=2300209

More informally, this course introduces the differential equations and expands on how one can approach (and, if possible, solve) these objects. We are interested in these objects because most of the known physics is expressed in terms of differential equations.

Who can/should take this course?

This is a must course for any undergraduate student in the department of Physics (see the curriculum) and the department of Physics Education (see the curriculum). Assuming that you have taken a sufficient set of prerequisite courses (see academic catalog), you should take this one too.

What are the course policies?

General:

  • You accept by taking this course that you will abide by the academic integrity rules within this course as well. You should definitely read my explanations of the expected academic integrity.
  • The medium of instruction in this course is English (article 5/1 in Regulations): you are of course free to use any language (Turkish, English, Quenya, etc.) when interacting with the teaching assistant or the instructor outside the classroom, but please do your best to stick to English inside the classroom!
  • Class attendance is mandatory by the Regulations (article 23/1). Although I am personally against mandatory attendance, I would strongly advice it anyway: Phys209 is a very essential course for the rest of your physics career and you may not be able to learn everything by studying yourself! That said, your attendance will not affect your grade.
  • The textbook for this class is “Elementary Differential Equations and Boundary Value Problems” by Boyce and Diprima (10th edition). You may also consult to “Advanced Calculus for Applications” (Hildebrand) and “Mathematical Methods in the Physical Sciences” (Boas) for supplementary reading.
  • There are some notes that I’ve been updating irregularly; you may check them here. For the source, see the files in github.
  • The midterm and final examinations along with their answers can be seen here: Midterm Examination, Final Examination

Homeworks:

  • Late homework submissions will be graded zero.
  • If a student receives the grade “zero” for six or more homeworks, s/he is no longer qualified to take the final exam: s/he gets the letter grade NA (article 24/5b in Regulations).
  • Students are free to type the homeworks on a computer, or write it down with pen and paper and then submit the scan of it digitally.
  • The homeworks are available as follows: HW1, HW2, HW3 HW4

Grades:

  • A student gets an NA if s/he misses both the midterm and the final (article 24/5b in Regulations).

  • The overall score for the course is calculated with the following formula:

    Score  = 0.6 ×max [mean (Grades of Any Ten Homeworks ) ] + 0.2 ×Final Grade + 0.2 ×Midterm Grade
  • As per my right provided by the Regulations (article 24/2), I will determine the conversion of Score to a Letter Grade at the end of the course: although there are various guidelines (say, assigning AA to the interval 90 − 100), I will use my judgment to determine the appropriate designations based on the overall class performance and the receptions of the exams by the class.

Make-up examinations:

  • If you have a medical report as an excuse for absenteeism in an examination, you are entitled to a make-up examination. I will decide on the contents and date of the make-up examination at my discreation, see Regulations (article 11/1c). Also, note that I will NOT accept medical reports directly presented to me: you need to submit it to Health and Guidance Center at the campus as required by the Regulations (article 10/2). Be aware that you should submit your report within 5 days after the period covered by the report ends.
  • You are not entitled to a make-up examination without a valid medical report as an excuse for absenteeism in an examination. Nevertheless, I may (or may not) grant you one if I believe you have a sufficiently valid reason: contact me if you have such a situation (a family crisis, death of a relative, etc.).
  • Make-up examination is harder than the corresponding regular exam by its nature: it may include more content and is usually conducted during the finals’ weeks. This is to ensure that a student does not get an unintended advantage by taking the make-up examination instead of the regular one, as they get more time for the exam than their peers in the class. Therefore, it is strategically unwise to try to get a make-up examination in an intend to get higher grades.

What will you learn in this course?

(1) Definition and Classification of Differential Equations: ordinary vs partial, linear vs non-linear, homogeneous vs non-homogeneous, 1st order vs 2nd order vs 3rd order vs … ⁡

(2) Linear equations with constant coefficients: Their relevance in physics and engineering, principle of superposition, characteristic equation, repeated roots, formal solution for both homogeneous and nonhomogeneous cases, initial and boundary value problems, the impulse response, the Laplace transform, convolution

(3) Linear equations with functional coefficients:

1.
Homogeneous solution: Their relevance in physics and engineering, known soluble cases, reduction of order, linear independence and Wronskian, series solutions (Frobenius Method), Fourier transforms and Fourier series
2.
Particular solution: Method of undetermined coefficients, method of variation of parameters

(4) Systems of first order linear differential equations: Conversion of arbitrary linear ordinary differential equations to systems of first order linear differential equations, analysis of coupled systems via matrix differential equations, phase space, formal solutions (Volterra integral equation, Dyson series)

(5) Eigensystems and Sturm-Liouville theory: Matrix diagonalization, normal modes, eigenvalues and eigenfunctions, Sturm-Liouville type differential equations and their solutions

(6) Beyond linear ordinary differential equations:

1.
Non-linear differential equations: Known soluble cases (separable, exact, Bernoulli)
2.
Partial differential equations: Separation of variables, Laplace equation, physical examples
