What is this course?

For a formal answer to this question, see

https://catalog.metu.edu.tr/course.php?course_code=2300209

and

https://catalog.metu.edu.tr/course.php?course_code=2300210

More informally, Phys209 introduces the differential equations and expands on how one can approach (and, if possible, solve) these objects whereas Phys210 introduces the vector calculus and the complex analysis. We are interested in these objects because most of the known physics is expressed in terms of differential equations whose solutions are organized using the language of vectors and complex numbers.

Who can/should take this course?

This is a must course for any undergraduate student in the department of Physics (see the curriculum) and the department of Physics Education (see the curriculum). Assuming that you have taken a sufficient set of prerequisite courses (see Phys209 and Phys210), you should take these courses too.

What are the course policies?

General:

  • You accept by taking this course that you will abide by the academic integrity rules within this course as well. You should definitely read my explanations of the expected academic integrity.
  • The medium of instruction in this course is English (article 5/1 in Regulations): you are of course free to use any language (Turkish, English, Quenya, etc.) when interacting with the instructor outside the classroom, but please do your best to stick to English inside the classroom!
  • Class attendance is mandatory by the Regulations (article 23/1). Although I am personally against mandatory attendance, I would strongly advice it anyway: Phys210 is a very essential course for the rest of your physics career and you may not be able to learn everything by studying yourself! That said, your attendance will not affect your grade.

Grades:

  • A student gets an NA if s/he takes none of the mid-term and final examinations (article 24/5b in Regulations).

  • The overall score for the course is calculated with the following formula:

    Score  = 100 102 × ( Midterm-1 Grade (out of 34 points) + Midterm-2 Grade (out of 34 points) + Final Grade (out of 34 points) )
  • As per my right provided by the Regulations (article 24/2), I will determine the conversion of Score to a Letter Grade at the end of the course: although there are various guidelines (say, assigning AA to the interval 90 − 100), I will use my judgment to determine the appropriate designations based on the overall class performance and the receptions of the exams by the class. For my grading strategy in past, check out my explanations here.

Make-up examinations:

  • If you have a medical report as an excuse for absenteeism in an examination, you are entitled to a make-up examination. I will decide on the contents and date of the make-up examination at my discreation, see Regulations (article 11/1c). Also, make sure to

    1.
    submit your medical report to Health and Guidance Center at the campus within 5 days after the period covered by the report ends, as required by the Regulations (article 10/2),
    2.
    send me a picture of the report as well.
  • You are not entitled to a make-up examination without a valid medical report as an excuse for absenteeism in an examination. Nevertheless, I may (or may not) grant you one if I believe you have a sufficiently valid reason: contact me if you have such a situation (a family crisis, death of a relative, etc.).
  • Make-up examination is harder than the corresponding regular exam by its nature: it may include more content and is usually conducted during the finals’ weeks. This is to ensure that a student does not get an unintended advantage by taking the make-up examination instead of the regular one, as they get more time for the exam than their peers in the class. Therefore, it is strategically unwise to try to get a make-up examination in an intend to get higher grades.

What will you learn in this course?

__________________________________________________________________________


Phys209

__________________________________________________________________________________

(1) Definition and Classification of Differential Equations: ordinary vs partial, linear vs non-linear, homogeneous vs non-homogeneous, 1st order vs 2nd order vs 3rd order vs …

(2) Linear equations with constant coefficients: Their relevance in physics and engineering, principle of superposition, characteristic equation, repeated roots, formal solution for both homogeneous and nonhomogeneous cases, initial and boundary value problems, the impulse response, the Laplace transform, convolution

(3) Linear equations with functional coefficients:

1.
Homogeneous solution: Their relevance in physics and engineering, known soluble cases, reduction of order, linear independence and Wronskian, series solutions (Frobenius Method), Fourier transforms and Fourier series
2.
Particular solution: Method of undetermined coefficients, method of variation of parameters

(4) Systems of first order linear differential equations: Conversion of arbitrary linear ordinary differential equations to systems of first order linear differential equations, analysis of coupled systems via matrix differential equations, phase space, formal solutions (Volterra integral equation, Dyson series)

(5) Eigensystems and Sturm-Liouville theory: Matrix diagonalization, normal modes, eigenvalues and eigenfunctions, Sturm-Liouville type differential equations and their solutions

(6) Beyond linear ordinary differential equations:

1.
Non-linear differential equations: Known soluble cases (separable, exact, Bernoulli)
2.
Partial differential equations: Separation of variables, Laplace equation, physical examples

__________________________________________


Phys210

__________________________________________________________________________________

(1) Definition and Fundamentals of Vector Spaces: basics of linear algebra and linear spaces, norms and inner product spaces, algebras over fields (tensor, exterior, alternating, and division algebras).

(2) Differentiation in Vector calculus: vector fields in Euclidean spaces, bivectors & covectors & forms, metric tensor, invariant formulation of differentiation in vectors (grad, div, curl, Laplacian), Helmholtz decomposition of vector fields

(3) Integration in Vector calculus: curves and line integrals, Frenet–Serret formulas, integral theorems (gradient theorem, divergence theorem, curl theorem, and Green’s theorem)

(4) Curvilinear Coordinate Transformations: coordinate charts, common orthogonal curvilinear coordinates (polar, cylindrical, spherical)

(5) Basics of Complex Calculus: analysis of complex numbers as a two dimensional real vector space (complex plane, geometric interpretations, polar form), algebraic properties of complex numbers, complex differentiation

(6) Complex Analysis: Riemann sphere, common Riemann surfaces, analyticity of complex functions, series expansions, analytic continuation and monodromy theorem
